Task 1
Cyber security careers are becoming more in demand and offer high salaries. There are many different jobs within the security industry, from offensive pentesting (hacking machines and reporting on vulnerabilities) to defensive security (defending against and investigating cyberattacks).
Why get a career in cyber:
High Pay - jobs in security have high starting salaries
Exciting - work can include legally hacking systems or defending against cyber attacks
Be in demand - there are over 3.5 million unfilled cyber positions

This lesson helps you break into cyber security by providing information about various cyber security roles; it also links to different learning paths that you can use to start building your cyber skills.
Task 2
A Security Analyst is a professional responsible for protecting an organization's computer systems, networks, and data from cyber threats. Their primary duties include monitoring security systems, detecting and responding to security incidents, analyzing vulnerabilities, and implementing security measures such as firewalls, intrusion detection systems (IDS), and antivirus software. They also perform risk assessments, maintain security policies, and ensure compliance with industry standards. Security Analysts play a critical role in preventing data breaches, minimizing cyber risks, and keeping an organization's digital assets safe.
Task 3
A Security Engineer is responsible for designing, building, and maintaining secure systems and networks. They develop and implement security solutions, such as firewalls, encryption protocols, and intrusion detection systems, to protect an organization's data and infrastructure. Security Engineers also identify vulnerabilities, conduct penetration testing, and work closely with IT teams to ensure systems are resilient against cyberattacks. Their focus is more on building and strengthening security architecture, whereas analysts focus more on monitoring and responding to threats.
Task 4
An Incident Reporter is responsible for documenting and reporting security incidents, breaches, or suspicious activities within an organization. They collect detailed information about the incident, log it according to standard procedures, and communicate it to security teams or management for investigation and response. Their role ensures that incidents are properly tracked, analyzed, and used to improve security measures.
Task 5
A Digital Forensics Examiner investigates cybercrimes and security incidents by collecting, analyzing, and preserving digital evidence from computers, networks, and electronic devices. They use specialized tools to recover deleted files, trace unauthorized access, and support legal or internal investigations. Their work ensures that evidence is accurate, admissible in court, and helps identify the source and impact of cyber incidents.
Task 6
A Malware Analyst studies and examines malicious software (malware) to understand how it works, spreads, and impacts systems. They reverse-engineer malware, identify its behavior and indicators of compromise, and provide insights to develop defenses, remove threats, and prevent future attacks. Their work is crucial for protecting organizations from viruses, ransomware, spyware, and other cyber threats.
Task 7
A Penetration Tester (or "ethical hacker") simulates cyberattacks on an organization's systems, networks, and applications to identify vulnerabilities before malicious hackers can exploit them. They perform tests, report security weaknesses, and recommend measures to strengthen defenses, ensuring the organization's infrastructure is secure against real-world attacks.
Task 8
A Red Teamer is a cybersecurity professional who simulates realistic cyberattacks to test an organization's defenses, including people, processes, and technology. They identify vulnerabilities, exploit weaknesses, and provide actionable recommendations to improve security, helping organizations prepare for real-world threats.

What I Learned & Why It's Useful in Security
Through this lesson on cyber security careers, I learned about various roles in the field and the unique contributions each makes to protecting organizations from cyber threats. I gained an understanding of both defensive and offensive roles and how they complement each other to maintain a strong security posture.
Security Analyst - Learned how analysts monitor systems, detect threats, and respond to incidents. This is useful because it helps prevent breaches and ensures continuous protection of data and networks.
Security Engineer - Learned how engineers design and implement secure systems and networks. Their work is useful for creating resilient infrastructure that can withstand attacks and reduce vulnerabilities.
Incident Reporter - Learned the importance of documenting and reporting security incidents. This is useful because accurate records help investigate issues, improve security measures, and maintain compliance.
Digital Forensics Examiner - Learned how examiners collect and analyze digital evidence. This is useful for investigating cybercrimes, understanding attack methods, and providing legal evidence.
Malware Analyst - Learned how malware analysts study malicious software to understand its behavior. This is useful in developing defenses, removing threats, and preventing future attacks.
Penetration Tester - Learned how ethical hackers simulate attacks to find vulnerabilities. This is useful for identifying weaknesses before malicious hackers exploit them, strengthening overall security.
Red Teamer - Learned how red teamers simulate realistic attacks on systems, people, and processes. This is useful for testing an organization's overall readiness and improving defenses against real-world threats.

Overall, this lesson showed me that cybersecurity is multi-faceted, requiring both proactive defenses and reactive measures. Understanding these roles is useful because it highlights how different professionals work together to protect digital assets, respond to incidents, and prevent cyberattacks, which is essential in today's technology-driven world.
